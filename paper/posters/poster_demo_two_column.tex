\postersection{Introduction}
Sentiment analysis is a fundamental NLP task, yet most evaluations benchmark a single model family on a single domain. Real-world deployment requires models that generalise across text styles, class granularities, and language patterns such as irony and financial jargon. We close this gap by comparing nine models from five families - lexicon, classical ML, deep learning, transformer, and generative LLM - across six public benchmarks. Beyond single-model evaluation, we test five hybrid combination strategies and use error-overlap analysis to explain when and why aggregation actually helps.

\postersection{Datasets}
Six public benchmarks spanning finance, reviews, and social media yield nine experimental configurations, mixing clean and noisy text, binary and multi-class settings, and explicit and subtle sentiment. Financial PhraseBank is evaluated as four agreement subsets (66\%, 75\%, All, and a merged All-agreement split) to test robustness to label noise.

Confidence-weighted voting is the most reliable strategy (best on 8/9 datasets, never degrades by more than 0.7 pp). On Twitter, excluding LLMs recovers +2.5 pp because all three generative models score below 0.573 there. The confidence cascade (threshold 0.90) routes most samples to cheaper models while staying competitive with the best single model, making it the best option when inference cost matters.

\postersection{Error Analysis}
We measure Jaccard similarity between each pair of models' error sets to quantify how much their mistakes overlap. High within-family overlap means that combining two models from the same family yields little diversity - they tend to fail on the same samples. Mean Jaccard is \textbf{0.414} within families versus \textbf{0.234} across families, confirming that heterogeneous ensembles succeed precisely because they bring genuinely different failure modes to the vote.

Finance-All has 0\% all-fail samples - every error is correctable by some model in the ensemble, making it the ideal case for voting. Yelp-Full (7.88\% all-fail) and TweetEval-Sentiment (3.37\%) contain samples involving sarcasm, mixed polarity, or fine-grained star ratings that defeat every model simultaneously. These extremely hard examples set a ceiling on what any fixed ensemble strategy can achieve, independent of how the votes are weighted.

\postersection{Key Takeaways}
\takeawayspanel{%
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0.38em,topsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{No single architecture is optimal across all domains.}
  \item \textbf{Finance datasets prefer transformers;} IMDB and irony favour LLMs; Twitter favours CNN and LSTM.
  \item \textbf{Confidence-weighted voting is the most reliable ensemble strategy,} outperforming the best single model on 8 of 9 datasets.
  \item \textbf{A 0.90 confidence cascade threshold gives the best practical balance} between quality and computational cost.
  \item \textbf{Hard examples and correlated errors set a hard ceiling} on what any fixed voting scheme can achieve.
  \item \textbf{Yelp-Full is the hardest dataset} (max accuracy 0.666): 7.88\% of samples defeat all nine models simultaneously, primarily due to fine-grained 5-class ratings.
\end{itemize}%
