\begin{abstract}
The evolution of Natural Language Processing has introduced a wide range of methods for sentiment analysis, ranging from traditional statistical classifiers to advanced Large Language Models. While LLMs demonstrate strong reasoning capabilities, their practical application is often challenged by computational costs and latency. This research presents a comprehensive comparative analysis of sentiment analysis techniques across a range of approaches, including lexicon-based methods, classical machine learning, encoder-only Transformers (DistilBERT , DistilRoBERTa), and modern generative models such as Gemini.

The study uses six distinct datasets - including Twitter, IMDB reviews, TweeEval benchmarks, Yelp, and the Financial PhraseBank - to evaluate model robustness across varying linguistic styles, from noisy social media content to formal financial reports. A central focus of this work is the exploration of hybrid strategies designed to combine the strengths of different model tiers. By implementing multi-stage decision-making frameworks, the research investigates how model consensus, uncertainty-driven escalation, and collaborative voting can enhance classification precision while managing resource allocation.

This paper evaluates cascading workflows in which smaller, specialized models interact with high-parameter LLMs to improve classification accuracy, particularly in resolving linguistic ambiguities such as irony. The study contributes to the development of more effective sentiment analysis pipelines, providing insights into how hybrid architectures can achieve higher predictive performance than individual models in complex, real-world data environments. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Sentiment analysis remains one of the most common tasks in natural language processing because it is useful in many practical settings, including opinion mining, customer feedback analysis, finance, and social media monitoring \cite{singh2020sentiment} \cite{sharma2025review}. At the same time, it is still a difficult problem. Performance depends not only on model quality, but also on domain, label scheme, text length, noise level, and the amount of context needed to interpret sentiment correctly. A model that works well on product reviews may perform much worse on short tweets, irony, or finance-oriented texts.

The range of available approaches has also expanded significantly. Classical machine learning methods remain attractive because they are simple, fast and easy to reproduce. Deep learning models and transformers often perform better than classical baselines on many widely used datasets, especially when enough task-specific training data is available. More recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have become a promising alternative because they can perform sentiment classification in zero-shot or few-shot settings and can adapt to different tasks through prompting \cite{zhang2024sentiment} \cite{liskowski2026large}. However, recent studies also show that LLMs do not consistently outperform other approaches across datasets, and that their results can vary with prompt design and with the domain of the evaluated data \cite{sa-in-age-of-genai} \cite{herrera2025overview}.

Previous work has already shown the value of these model families, but it often studies them separately. Many papers compare models within one group, such as classical or deep-learning baselines, while more recent work focuses specifically on LLM prompting or LLM-only aggregation \cite{sun2023sentiment} \cite{zhang2024sentiment}. What is still less explored is a unified comparison across all major model families together with a practical analysis of hybrid strategies. In particular, there is still a need for studies that ask not only which models are most accurate, but also whether they make complementary errors, whether confidence can be used to route difficult cases, and when simple voting is likely to help.

This paper addresses that gap. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a multidomain comparison of nine models from four families: classical ML, deep learning, transformers, and LLMs - evaluated on six datasets spanning finance, reviews, and social media,
    \item an evaluation of three hybrid strategies (LLM-only voting, confidence-based hierarchical escalation, and cross-family ensemble voting), with analysis of both performance gains and the cost-quality trade-off,
    \item an error-overlap and hard-example analysis showing when models make complementary errors and when correlated failures limit what aggregation can achieve.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Recent survey work by Sharma et al.\ \cite{sharma2025review} and Singh et al.\ \cite{singh2020sentiment} gives a broad account of SA techniques across domains, confirming that no single approach dominates: classical models such as SVM with TF-IDF remain competitive on short or well-structured text, while neural models gain an advantage when more training data is available and when longer context matters.

The role of LLMs in sentiment classification has been studied directly in several recent papers. Zhang et al.\ \cite{zhang2024sentiment} conduct a large-scale evaluation of instruction-tuned LLMs across standard SA benchmarks and find that, while LLMs are competitive with fine-tuned models on many tasks, they still fall short in domain-specific settings such as finance. Krugmann and Hartmann \cite{sa-in-age-of-genai} show that generative models are sensitive to prompt wording and can refuse to classify content that triggers safety filters, which introduces variability not present in supervised models. Herrera-Poyatos et al.\ \cite{herrera2025overview} review this uncertainty further, identifying prompt sensitivity and output inconsistency as key open problems and calling for mitigation strategies. On the more advanced end, Liskowski and Jankowski \cite{liskowski2026large} apply reasoning-infused LLMs to aspect-based SA and show gains when models are prompted to explain their decisions before labelling.

Hybrid and ensemble strategies have also received growing attention. Sun et al.\ \cite{sun2023sentiment} propose having multiple LLM agents negotiate a final label and report that this reduces individual model errors. Ranjbar and Baghbani \cite{ranjbar2025lotus} combine a fine-tuned RoBERTa classifier with Llama-generated explanations at SemEval-2025, achieving stronger results than either component alone. These results suggest that combining model families is promising, but neither study examines whether performance gains hold across diverse domains or when cheaper models can absorb most of the traffic.

\textbf{IMDB Large Movie Review Dataset} \cite{maas2011imdb} contains 50{,}000 movie reviews split evenly into training and test sets. Each review is labelled as either positive or negative. The dataset is well-studied and represents a relatively clean, long-form binary classification task.

\textbf{Financial PhraseBank} \cite{malo2014phrasebank} consists of 13{,}000 sentences extracted from financial news articles and annotated by 16 domain experts as positive, negative, or neutral. The dataset is available in four subsets defined by the minimum annotator agreement required to include a sentence: 50\%, 66\%, 75\%, and 100\% (all-agree). Higher agreement thresholds produce smaller but less ambiguous subsets. We evaluated on all four subsets to examine how label quality affects model performance.

\textbf{Twitter Entity Sentiment Analysis} \cite{twitter_kaggle} is a Kaggle dataset of tweets labelled with four categories: positive, negative, neutral, and irrelevant. Tweets are short and informal, which makes this dataset a challenging benchmark for models trained primarily on longer, cleaner text.

\textbf{Yelp} \cite{zhang2015character} contains customer reviews from the Yelp platform with ratings on a five-point scale (1-5 stars). We use a subset of the full dataset with 30{,}000 training and 5{,}000 test samples, it is the only dataset with five sentiment classes, making it particularly useful for evaluating fine-grained sentiment discrimination.

\textbf{TweetEval Sentiment and TweetEval Irony} \cite{barbieri2020tweeteval} are part of the TweetEval unified benchmark for tweet classification. The sentiment subset uses three classes (negative, neutral, positive) and the irony subset is binary (ironic vs.\ non-ironic). Including the irony task helps us check if models can understand more subtle language, not just basic positive or negative sentiment.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Model Families}

We evaluate nine models across five paradigms: lexicon-based, classical ML, deep learning, transformer-based, and LLMs.

\textbf{Lexicon-based model:}  
We include VADER, a rule-based sentiment analyzer that relies on a curated lexicon and heuristic rules. It serves as a lightweight baseline requiring no training.

\textbf{Classical machine learning:}  
We use a TF-IDF vectorizer combined with a Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier. This approach represents text as sparse features and has historically been a strong baseline for sentiment classification.

\textbf{Deep learning models:}  
We evaluate two neural architectures:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{CNN}: captures local n-gram patterns via convolutional filters.
\item \textbf{LSTM}: models sequential dependencies and contextual information in text.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Transformer-based models:}  
We include DistilBERT and DistilRoBERTa, which are lightweight variants of large pretrained transformers. These models are fine-tuned on each dataset and represent the state of the art in supervised sentiment analysis.

\textbf{Large Language Models (LLMs):}  
We evaluate three instruction-tuned LLMs accessed via API:
\begin{itemize}
\item Gemini (gemini-2.5-flash)
\item Mistral (mistral-small-2503)
\item Llama (llama-4-maverick-17b-128e-instruct)
\end{itemize}
These models are used in a few-shot setting without task-specific fine-tuning.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate model performance using two standard metrics:

\textbf{Accuracy} measures the proportion of correctly classified samples:
\[
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{\text{Number of correct predictions}}{\text{Total number of samples}}
\]

\textbf{Macro-averaged F1-score} accounts for class imbalance by computing the F1-score independently for each class and averaging the results:
\[
F1_{\text{macro}} = \frac{1}{C} \sum_{i=1}^{C} F1_i
\]
where $C$ is the number of classes.

These metrics provide complementary perspectives: accuracy reflects overall correctness, while macro-F1 ensures that minority classes are treated equally.

\subsection{Ensemble and Hybrid Strategies}

To explore the benefits of combining models, we evaluate several aggregation strategies:

\textbf{Majority voting:}  
Each model contributes one vote, and the most frequent label is selected.

\textbf{Accuracy-weighted voting:}  
Votes are weighted by each model's overall accuracy on the dataset.

\textbf{Confidence-weighted voting:}  
Predictions are weighted by model confidence scores. For LLMs, which do not provide calibrated probabilities, a fallback confidence estimate is used if needed (for example, overall accuracy on the dataset).

\textbf{Confidence-based hybrid pipeline:}  
Models are organized in a hierarchical sequence from low-cost to high-cost (SVM $\rightarrow$ CNN $\rightarrow$ LSTM $\rightarrow$ transformer $\rightarrow$ LLM). A sample is classified by the first model whose confidence exceeds a predefined threshold; otherwise, it is passed to the next model in the chain.

This design allows efficient allocation of computational resources while preserving accuracy on difficult examples.

\subsection{Error Analysis Methodology}

To better understand model behavior, we perform an error-overlap analysis based on the Jaccard similarity of misclassified sample sets. For two models $A$ and $B$, the similarity is defined as:
\[
J(A, B) = \frac{|E_A \cap E_B|}{|E_A \cup E_B|}
\]
where $E_A$ and $E_B$ are the sets of samples misclassified by each model.

We also identify \textit{hard samples}, defined as those misclassified by at least 60\% of models, and \textit{all-fail samples}, misclassified by all models. These metrics provide insight into dataset difficulty and the limits of ensemble methods.

\section{LLM-Based Sentiment Classification Setup}
The classification setup was designed to evaluate the capabilities of state-of-the-art LLMs. All models were accessed through the Google Cloud Vertex AI API, which provided a unified infrastructure for request management and model serving. The study included three models:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gemini}: gemini-2.5-flash,
    \item \textbf{Mistral}: mistral-small-2503,
    \item \textbf{Llama}: llama-4-maverick-17b-128e-instruct-maas.
\end{itemize}

The classification process was handled by a Python-based integration layer, and outputs were stored in structured JSONL format to keep predictions aligned with ground-truth labels through unique sample indices.
\subsection{Prompt Engineering and In-Context Learning}
To reduce variation across model responses, a standardized five-part prompt structure was used. It was designed to give the models clear task instructions and a consistent input format:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{System Role}: Sets the model's role as a sentiment classifier,
\item \textbf{Data Context}: Specifies the source of the text, for example financial reports,
\item \textbf{Category Definitions}: Provides a fixed mapping of labels to numeric or categorical values,
\item \textbf{Few-Shot Examples}: Provides a small number of labeled examples to help the model calibrate its decisions, which is especially useful for noisy datasets,
\item \textbf{Target Input}: Contains the records to be classified in a consistent format.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Error Handling and Retries}
Large-scale API-based inference introduces challenges regarding rate limits and safety-alignment triggers. During the initial pass, we observed a non-negligible rate of unclassified examples (Table~\ref{tab:unclassified_by_model}), primarily due to:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rate Limiting}: Exceeding tokens-per-minute (TPM) or requests-per-minute (RPM) quotas,
    \item \textbf{Content Filtering}: Safety filters occasionally blocked prompts containing vulgar or highly emotional review fragments, even when they were relevant to the sentiment classification task.
\end{enumerate}

To reduce the number of missing predictions, a simple retry procedure was used. For rate-limit errors, requests were repeated with increasing delays. For samples that failed inside a batch, problematic items were resubmitted individually. \section{Multi-Model Voting and Tie-Breaking Strategy}
To improve our classifications, we combined the predictions from all three models (Gemini, Llama and Mistral) using a majority voting logic. For every text sample, the final label was chosen based on the most frequent prediction among the models.

This approach is inspired by earlier work on multi-agent LLM sentiment analysis \cite{sun2023sentiment}, where several model responses are generated for the same input and the final label is chosen by majority decision.

In cases where all three models provided different labels (a total disagreement), we applied a fixed priority rule to resolve the tie. The priority was based on the overall performance of the models during our tests:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gemini} (Highest priority),
    \item \textbf{Llama},
    \item \textbf{Mistral}.
\end{enumerate}
This ensures that whenever a consensus is not reached, the system relies on the prediction from the model that proved most reliable across the majority of our datasets.

\subsection{Summary of LLM-Based Results}

The experiments demonstrate that while individual Large Language Models are highly effective for sentiment analysis, their performance varies significantly across different domains. The voting approach brought improvements on several structured datasets, especially in the finance domain, but these improvements were relatively small.

However, we observed that on noisier data (e.g., social media), simple voting does not always outperform the best single model. It is important to note that the results presented here are highly dependent on the quality of the instructions provided to the models. Further refinement of prompt engineering-specifically through better selection of few-shot examples and more detailed category descriptions-could likely lead to even higher classification accuracy across all tested domains.

\section{Multi-Model Confidence Voting}
In real-world applications of sentiment analysis, predictive performance is not the sole consideration. Operational efficiency - specifically the costs associated with hardware requirements and inference throughput - is equally critical. Large Language Models (LLMs) and other Transformer-based architectures, such as BERT or RoBERTa, are significantly more resource-intensive than traditional approaches like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) \cite{zhang2024sentiment,sa-in-age-of-genai}.

To mitigate these costs, a hybrid framework is proposed to maximize the utilization of computationally efficient models for trivial cases while reserving more powerful models for challenging ones. The proposed system utilizes the confidence scores produced by each model during inference. Models are organized hierarchically, from the least to the most computationally expensive:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item SVM,
    \item CNN,
    \item LSTM,
    \item RoBERTa,
    \item Gemini.
\end{enumerate}
This ordering ensures that the more expensive Transformer models are positioned at the end of the chain. In theory, this ensures that only the most complex cases are processed by these high-cost models, while simpler instances are classified by traditional architectures.

Because the preceding models successfully classified 'clear' instances with high confidence, the terminal model was exclusively tasked with the 'residuals': samples characterized by extreme ambiguity, linguistic noise, or domain-specific complexity that challenged all previous architectures in the pipeline.

\section{Cross-Model Ensemble Voting}
\label{sec:cross_ensemble}

The LLM-only voting framework demonstrates the potential of prediction aggregation, but it leaves an open question: does incorporating the full spectrum of model families, classical ML, deep learning, transformers, and LLMs-yield further improvements? To answer this, we extend the voting framework to all nine architectures evaluated in this benchmark. The motivation is that different models make different mistakes, so when used together, they can balance out each other’s weaknesses.

\subsection{Voting Strategies and Implementation}
We evaluate four ensemble strategies applied row-by-row across the nine models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Majority Voting}: each model contributes one vote; the most frequent label wins. Ties are resolved by random selection (fixed seed),
    \item \textbf{Accuracy-Weighted Voting} (raw): each model's vote is weighted by its overall accuracy on the dataset. The label accumulating the highest total weight is selected,
    \item \textbf{Confidence-Weighted Voting}: each vote is weighted by the model's predicted confidence score for that specific sample. Since LLMs do not expose calibrated probabilities, they receive a fallback weight equal to the mean confidence of the ML models on the same dataset,
    \item \textbf{Majority Voting (ML\,+\,Transformer only)}: Gemini, Llama, and Mistral are excluded; majority voting is applied over the six remaining models.
\end{enumerate}

Accuracy-weighted voting was also evaluated with softmax-normalised weights, but since all nine model accuracies fall within a moderate range, the softmax compression reduces weights to near-uniform values ($\approx$0.11 per model), producing results identical to raw accuracy weighting. We therefore report only the raw variant.

The DistilRoBERTa transformer is the strongest single model on the finance dataset, where fine-tuned language representations provide a clear advantage. Gemini performs best on IMDB and TweetEval-Irony, tasks that rely on broad world knowledge and subtle language understanding. \subsection{Domain-Specific Effects of LLM Inclusion}

The Twitter dataset exposes LLM limitations: all three LLMs score between 0.515 and 0.573, far below CNN (0.866) and LSTM (0.853). This difference is caused by the Twitter dataset's four-class labeling scheme based on emotional intensity rather than simple sentiment polarity-a distinction that LLMs, trained on broad world knowledge, handle less reliably than task-specific classifiers. When LLMs are included in the majority vote, their poorly calibrated predictions drown out the signal from stronger models. Excluding them (Majority Voting, no LLM) recovers a gain of +2.5 pp over the best individual model, a result consistent with the findings of \cite{sa-in-age-of-genai} which show that generative models do not always generalise well across different sentiment labeling schemes.

On the finance datasets, the relationship reverses: LLMs rank among the top three models, and including them in confidence-weighted voting is beneficial. This suggests that the optimal ensemble composition is task-dependent: LLMs should be included when their domain competence is confirmed, and excluded otherwise.

\section{Cross-Model Error Overlap Analysis}
\label{sec:error_overlap}

The results presented so far show that the tested methods improve performance only in some settings, and in most cases the gains are not large. To better understand these limits, we analyse \emph{where} the models fail rather than only \emph{how often} they fail. For each dataset, we therefore measure pairwise overlap between model error sets using the Jaccard similarity coefficient, compute the distribution of samples misclassified by multiple models simultaneously, and inspect the hardest examples on which most or all classifiers fail. This analysis serves two purposes: it helps explain why the tested methods do not always lead to clear improvements, and it provides additional context for thinking about more selective hybrid strategies.

\subsection{Error Overlap Within and Across Model Families}

The first recurring pattern is that models from the same family tend to fail on the same instances substantially more often than models from different families. Across all datasets, the mean Jaccard similarity of error sets is 0.414 for within-family pairs, compared with only 0.234 for cross-family pairs. This confirms that architectural similarity translates into partially redundant error profiles. The effect is visible in every domain, but is strongest for Yelp-Full, TweetEval-Sentiment, and Twitter, where the average within-family overlap reaches 0.556, 0.519, and 0.472 respectively.

Figure~\ref{fig:mean_pairwise_jaccard} visualises this effect directly. Three blocks are clearly visible: the transformer pair (DistilBERT, DistilRoBERTa), the deep-learning/classical cluster around CNN, SVM, and LSTM, and the LLM cluster formed by Gemini, Llama, and Mistral. By contrast, overlap values between these families are markedly lower, which supports the interpretation that ensemble gains depend on combining models with genuinely different error profiles.

At the level of individual model pairs, the most pronounced overlaps are observed for DistilBERT--DistilRoBERTa on Yelp-Full (0.784), Llama--Mistral on Twitter (0.680), and Gemini--Llama on Twitter (0.613). These values indicate that adding more models from the same family does not necessarily increase ensemble diversity. In other words, a larger ensemble is not automatically a more complementary ensemble.

At the same time, several high-overlap cross-family pairs are also observed, most notably SVM--LSTM on TweetEval-Sentiment (0.611) and CNN--SVM on Finance-66Agree (0.590). This is an important nuance: some examples are difficult not because one model family is uniquely weak, but because the underlying samples are intrinsically ambiguous and induce correlated failures even across heterogeneous architectures.

\subsection{Dataset Difficulty and Hard Examples}

Table~\ref{tab:error_overlap_summary} also reveals large differences in dataset-level difficulty. Yelp-Full is the most difficult dataset in this group: 32.84\% of samples are misclassified by at least 60\% of models, and 7.88\% are misclassified by all nine classifiers simultaneously. TweetEval-Sentiment ranks second, with 21.39\% hard samples and 3.37\% all-fail cases. In contrast, the finance datasets, particularly Finance-All-Agree, contain relatively few universally difficult examples. This helps explain why the finance datasets benefit the most from aggregation: there are enough disagreements to exploit, but comparatively fewer samples on which the entire pool fails together.

The qualitative analysis of the hardest samples indicates that the most problematic cases are linguistically and semantically non-trivial. In TweetEval-Irony and TweetEval-Sentiment, many all-fail examples rely on polarity reversal, sarcasm, understatement, or sentiment expressed through pragmatics rather than explicit lexical cues. In Yelp-Full, the hardest reviews are typically long, mixed-polarity texts that combine praise and criticism and require fine-grained rating decisions rather than binary sentiment detection. The Twitter dataset introduces a different source of difficulty: short, noisy, context-poor posts containing placeholders, slang, or fragmentary utterances. These observations suggest that large-scale error overlap is often driven by genuine ambiguity of the input rather than by isolated model weaknesses.

\subsection{Implications for Hybrid System Design}

The error-overlap analysis has direct consequences for the design of hybrid sentiment classifiers. First, high within-family overlap means smaller gains from combining multiple similar models, since they frequently contribute redundant mistakes. Second, the existence of hard examples shared across nearly all architectures shows why fixed voting over a static pool cannot always outperform the best individual model: when most models fail on the same sample, majority aggregation has little corrective capacity.

These findings motivate a more selective hybrid strategy than uniform voting. The results reported earlier already suggest that the best model family is dataset-dependent: transformers perform best on the finance datasets, LLMs are strongest on IMDB and TweetEval-Irony, and deep learning models are clearly superior on Twitter. Rather than treating all predictions as equally useful, future hybrid methods should take into account both expected model quality and the degree of error correlation between model families. In this sense, error-overlap analysis is not only descriptive; it helps identify the conditions under which more adaptive hybrid architectures may be worth exploring.

\section{Summary}
This benchmarking study provides a multidimensional evaluation of sentiment classification architectures, ranging from classical support vector machines to state-of-the-art large language models. Our results lead to four primary conclusions regarding the current state of automated sentiment analysis:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Domain Sensitivity over Model Scale}: While Large Language Models like Gemini and Llama-4 demonstrate superior performance on linguistically complex tasks such as irony detection and long-form movie reviews, they are frequently outperformed by task-specific architectures (CNNs and LSTMs) on noisy, colloquial datasets like Twitter. This suggests that "scaling up" is not a universal solution for all sentiment domains.

    \item \textbf{Hybrid Confidence-based Voting Framework}: We demonstrated that a hierarchical confidence-based voting framework - routing "easy" samples to SVMs and "hard" cases to Gemini - can significantly reduce computational overhead and API costs. By utilizing a confidence threshold of 0.90, we maintained competitive accuracy while offloading the majority of the workload to efficient, non-generative models.

    \item \textbf{Cross-model Ensemble}: Our cross-model ensemble analysis reveals that the most effective voting systems are those that combine heterogeneous architectures. Because models within the same family (e.g., LLMs or Transformers) have high error overlap (Jaccard similarity $> 0.4$), ensemble gains are maximized when pooling predictions across different families rather than simply stacking more models of the same type.

    \item \textbf{Dataset Resilience}: The finance and IMDB datasets proved to be the most "correctable" through voting, whereas social media data (Yelp-Full and Twitter) exhibited high rates of "all-fail" samples. This indicates that current bottlenecks in sentiment analysis are often driven by inherent linguistic ambiguity and labeling noise rather than specific model architectural failures.
\end{enumerate}

In conclusion, the optimal deployment strategy for sentiment analysis remains highly context-dependent.
