\section{Introduction}
Sentiment analysis remains one of the most common tasks in natural language processing because it is useful in many practical settings, including opinion mining, customer feedback analysis, finance, and social media monitoring \cite{singh2020sentiment} \cite{sharma2025review}. At the same time, it is still a difficult problem. Performance depends not only on model quality, but also on domain, label scheme, text length, noise level, and the amount of context needed to interpret sentiment correctly. A model that works well on product reviews may perform much worse on short tweets, irony, or finance-oriented texts.

The range of available approaches has also expanded significantly. Classical machine learning methods remain attractive because they are simple, fast and easy to reproduce. Deep learning models and transformers often perform better than classical baselines on many benchmark datasets, especially when enough task-specific training data is available. More recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have become a promising alternative because they can perform sentiment classification in zero-shot or few-shot settings and can adapt to different tasks through prompting \cite{zhang2024sentiment} \cite{liskowski2026large}. However, recent studies also show that LLMs do not consistently outperform other approaches across datasets, and that their results can vary with prompt design and with the domain of the evaluated data \cite{sa-in-age-of-genai} \cite{herrera2025overview}.

Previous work has already shown the value of these model families, but it often studies them separately. Many papers compare models within one group, such as classical or deep-learning baselines, while more recent work focuses specifically on LLM prompting or LLM-only aggregation \cite{sun2023sentiment} \cite{zhang2024sentiment}. What is still less explored is a unified comparison across all major model families together with a practical analysis of hybrid strategies. In particular, there is still a need for studies that ask not only which models are most accurate, but also whether they make complementary errors, whether confidence can be used to route difficult cases, and when simple voting is likely to help.

This paper addresses that problem by benchmarking nine sentiment classification models from four families: classical ML, deep learning, transformers, and LLMs. The evaluation covers datasets from multiple domains, including finance, reviews, and social media. The study then considers three hybrid strategies: LLM-only voting, a confidence-based hierarchical scheme that uses confidence thresholds to decide whether a sample should be passed to a stronger model, and heterogeneous ensemble voting across all model families. Finally, the analysis examines how model errors overlap across datasets and inspects hard examples that are misclassified by many models at once. This makes it possible to answer three practical questions: when does combining models actually help, when can confidence be used to balance quality and cost, and when do correlated errors limit the value of simple aggregation.

The main contributions of this work are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a multidomain benchmark comparing classical ML, deep learning, transformers, and LLMs under a unified evaluation setup,
    \item an evaluation of three hybrid strategies: LLM-only voting, confidence-based hierarchical voting, and cross-family ensemble voting, measuring both performance gains and the practical trade-off between stronger and cheaper models,
    \item an error-overlap and hard-example analysis showing when different models are genuinely complementary and when they fail in similar ways.
\end{itemize}

\section{LLM-Based Sentiment Classification Setup}
The inference pipeline was designed to evaluate the capabilities of state-of-the-art LLMs in a controlled, reproducible environment. All models were accessed through the \textbf{Google Cloud Vertex AI API}, which provided a unified infrastructure for request management and model serving. We utilized three distinct architectures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gemini}: gemini-2.5-flash
    \item \textbf{Mistral}: mistral-small-2503
    \item \textbf{Llama}: llama-4-maverick-17b-128e-instruct-maas
\end{itemize}

The classification process was orchestrated via a Python-based integration layer, capturing outputs in structured JSONL format to ensure data integrity and facilitate precise alignment with ground-truth labels through unique sample indices.
\subsection{Prompt Engineering and In-Context Learning}
To minimize variance across model responses, we employed a standardized, five-block prompt architecture. This structure leverages In-Context Learning (ICL) principles to provide the models with both semantic boundaries and formatting constraints:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{System Role}: Establishes the persona of a domain-expert sentiment classifier, optimizing the model's internal attention mechanisms for the task.
\item \textbf{Data Context}: Mitigates domain-shift issues by specifying the source of the text (np. financial reports vs. social media).
\item \textbf{Category Definitions}: Provides a rigid mapping of labels to numeric or categorical values, reducing lexical ambiguity.
\item \textbf{Few-Shot Examples}: Serves as a calibration mechanism. By including curated positive and negative snippets, we anchor the decision boundary, which is particularly critical for noisy datasets like Twitter.
\item \textbf{Target Input}: Appends the batch of records for inference, utilizing a clear separator to prevent prompt injection or context leakage.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pipeline Resilience and Error Handling}
Large-scale API-based inference introduces challenges regarding rate limits and safety-alignment triggers. During the initial pass, we observed a non-negligible rate of unclassified examples (Table~\ref{tab:unclassified_by_model}), primarily due to:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rate Limiting}: Exceeding tokens-per-minute (TPM) or requests-per-minute (RPM) quotas.
    \item \textbf{Content Filtering}: False positives in safety filters where benign text (e.g., intense movie reviews on IMDb) was flagged as prohibited content.
\end{enumerate}

To ensure a complete dataset for benchmarking, we implemented a multi-stage recovery framework. This included an exponential backoff strategy for rate limits and a secondary "atomic-retry" pass for samples that failed within batches (re-submitting blocked batch-items as individual requests). As shown in Table~\ref{tab:unclassified_by_model_after_retry}, this methodology reduced the missing data to a statistically insignificant level (near-zero loss).

\section{Multi-Model Voting and Tie-Breaking Strategy}
To improve the stability of our classifications, we combined the predictions from all three models (Gemini, Llama and Mistral) using a majority voting logic. For every text sample, the final label was chosen based on the most frequent prediction among the models.

This approach is inspired by the multi-agent negotiation frameworks proposed in literature \cite{sun2023sentiment}, where multiple LLM outputs are generated through role-flipped negotiations and a final sentiment polarity is determined by selecting the most frequent decision.

In cases where all three models provided different labels (a total disagreement), we applied a fixed priority rule to resolve the tie. The priority was based on the overall performance of the models during our tests:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gemini} (Highest priority)
    \item \textbf{Llama}
    \item \textbf{Mistral}
\end{enumerate}
This ensures that whenever a consensus is not reached, the system relies on the prediction from the model that proved most reliable across the majority of our datasets.

\subsection{Summary of LLM-Based Results}

The experiments demonstrate that while individual Large Language Models are highly effective for sentiment analysis, their performance varies significantly across different domains. Our voting approach proved beneficial for structured datasets, such as financial reports, where it consistently improved both Accuracy and F1-scores by filtering out random errors from single models.

However, we observed that on noisier data (e.g., social media), simple voting does not always outperform the best single model. It is important to note that the results presented here are highly dependent on the quality of the instructions provided to the models. Further refinement of prompt engineering-specifically through better selection of few-shot examples and more detailed category descriptions-could likely lead to even higher classification accuracy across all tested domains.

\section{Multi-Model Confidence Voting}
In real-world applications of sentiment analysis, predictive performance is not the sole consideration. Operational efficiency - specifically the costs associated with hardware requirements and inference throughput - is equally critical. Large Language Models (LLMs) and other Transformer-based architectures, such as BERT or RoBERTa, are significantly more resource-intensive than traditional approaches like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) \cite{zhang2024sentiment,sa-in-age-of-genai}.

To mitigate these costs, a hybrid framework is proposed to maximize the utilization of computationally efficient models for trivial cases while reserving more powerful models for challenging ones. The proposed system utilizes the confidence scores produced by each model during inference. Models are organized hierarchically, from the least to the most computationally expensive:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item SVM
    \item CNN
    \item LSTM
    \item RoBERTa
    \item Gemini
\end{enumerate}
This ordering ensures that the more expensive Transformer models are positioned at the end of the chain. In theory, this ensures that only the most complex cases are processed by these high-cost models, while simpler instances are classified by traditional architectures.

The framework was evaluated using three confidence thresholds: 0.90, 0.95, and 0.98.

Because the preceding models successfully classified 'clear' instances with high confidence, the terminal model was exclusively tasked with the 'residuals': samples characterized by extreme ambiguity, linguistic noise, or domain-specific complexity that challenged all previous architectures in the pipeline.

\section{Cross-Model Ensemble Voting}
\label{sec:cross_ensemble}

The LLM-only voting framework presented in Section~IV demonstrates the potential of prediction aggregation, but it leaves an open question: does incorporating the full spectrum of model families-classical ML, deep learning, transformers, and LLMs-yield further improvements? To answer this, we extend the voting framework to all nine architectures evaluated in this benchmark: DistilBERT, DistilRoBERTa, CNN, VADER, TF-IDF\,+\,SVM, LSTM, Gemini, Llama, and Mistral. The motivation is that heterogeneous models exhibit complementary failure modes and, when combined, can compensate for each other's systematic biases.

\subsection{Voting Strategies and Implementation}
We evaluate four ensemble strategies applied row-by-row across the nine models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Majority Voting}: each model contributes one vote; the most frequent label wins. Ties are resolved by random selection (fixed seed).
    \item \textbf{Accuracy-Weighted Voting} (raw): each model's vote is weighted by its overall accuracy on the dataset. The label accumulating the highest total weight is selected.
    \item \textbf{Confidence-Weighted Voting}: each vote is weighted by the model's predicted confidence score for that specific sample. Since LLMs do not expose calibrated probabilities, they receive a fallback weight equal to the mean confidence of the ML models on the same dataset.
    \item \textbf{Majority Voting (ML\,+\,Transformer only)}: Gemini, Llama, and Mistral are excluded; majority voting is applied over the six remaining models.
\end{enumerate}

Accuracy-weighted voting was also evaluated with softmax-normalised weights, but since all nine model accuracies fall within a moderate range, the softmax compression reduces weights to near-uniform values ($\approx$0.11 per model), producing results identical to raw accuracy weighting. We therefore report only the raw variant.

\subsection{Individual Model Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:all_models_acc} reports the accuracy of each model across all nine datasets. Several domain-dependent patterns are immediately apparent. The DistilRoBERTa transformer is the strongest single model on the finance corpora, where fine-tuned language representations provide a clear advantage. Gemini leads on IMDB and TweetEval-Irony-tasks that benefit from broad world knowledge and nuanced semantic understanding. Conversely, on the Twitter dataset, the CNN and LSTM architectures outperform both transformers and LLMs by a notable margin; the concise, colloquial nature of tweets appears to favour pattern-based feature extractors over deep generative priors. VADER is consistently the weakest classifier across all domains, with near-chance performance on multi-class datasets such as Twitter (0.220) and Yelp-Full (0.300).

\subsection{Domain-Specific Effects of LLM Inclusion}

The Twitter dataset exposes a critical failure mode: all three LLMs score between 0.515 and 0.573, far below CNN (0.866) and LSTM (0.853). The source of this discrepancy is the Twitter dataset's three-class labeling scheme based on emotional intensity rather than simple sentiment polarity-a distinction that LLMs, trained on broad world knowledge, handle less reliably than task-specific classifiers. When LLMs are included in the majority vote, their poorly calibrated predictions drown out the signal from stronger models. Excluding them (Majority Voting, no LLM) recovers a gain of +2.5 pp over the best individual model, a result consistent with the findings of \cite{sa-in-age-of-genai} that generative models do not universally generalise across sentiment labeling conventions.

On the finance corpora the relationship reverses: LLMs rank among the top three models, and including them in confidence-weighted voting is beneficial. This suggests that the optimal ensemble composition is task-dependent: LLMs should be included when their domain competence is confirmed, and excluded otherwise.

\section{Cross-Model Error Overlap Analysis}
\label{sec:error_overlap}

The ensemble results above show that prediction aggregation is beneficial only in selected settings. To explain this behaviour, we analyse \emph{where} the models fail rather than only \emph{how often} they fail. For each dataset, we therefore measure pairwise overlap between model error sets using the Jaccard similarity coefficient, compute the distribution of samples misclassified by multiple models simultaneously, and inspect the hardest examples on which most or all classifiers fail. This analysis serves two purposes: it explains the mixed effectiveness of the tested voting schemes and provides a design rationale for more selective hybrid strategies such as confidence-based escalation.

\subsection{Error Overlap Within and Across Model Families}

The first recurring pattern is that models from the same family tend to fail on the same instances substantially more often than models from different families. Across all datasets, the mean Jaccard similarity of error sets is 0.414 for within-family pairs, compared with only 0.234 for cross-family pairs. This confirms that architectural similarity translates into partially redundant error profiles. The effect is visible in every domain, but is strongest for Yelp-Full, TweetEval-Sentiment, and Twitter, where the average within-family overlap reaches 0.556, 0.519, and 0.472 respectively.

Figure~\ref{fig:mean_pairwise_jaccard} visualises this effect directly. Three blocks are clearly visible: the transformer pair (DistilBERT, DistilRoBERTa), the deep-learning/classical cluster around CNN, SVM, and LSTM, and the LLM cluster formed by Gemini, Llama, and Mistral. By contrast, overlap values between these families are markedly lower, which supports the interpretation that ensemble gains depend on combining models with genuinely different error profiles.

\caption{Mean pairwise Jaccard similarity of model error sets, averaged across all datasets. Darker cells indicate stronger overlap in the sets of misclassified samples.}

At the level of individual model pairs, the most pronounced overlaps are observed for DistilBERT--DistilRoBERTa on Yelp-Full (0.784), Llama--Mistral on Twitter (0.680), and Gemini--Llama on Twitter (0.613). These values indicate that adding more models from the same family does not necessarily increase ensemble diversity. In other words, a larger ensemble is not automatically a more complementary ensemble.

At the same time, several high-overlap cross-family pairs are also observed, most notably SVM--LSTM on TweetEval-Sentiment (0.611) and CNN--SVM on Finance-66Agree (0.590). This is an important nuance: some examples are difficult not because one model family is uniquely weak, but because the underlying samples are intrinsically ambiguous and induce correlated failures even across heterogeneous architectures.

\subsection{Dataset Difficulty and Hard Examples}

Table~\ref{tab:error_overlap_summary} also reveals large differences in dataset-level difficulty. Yelp-Full is the most challenging corpus by a wide margin: 32.84\% of samples are misclassified by at least 60\% of models, and 7.88\% are misclassified by all nine classifiers simultaneously. TweetEval-Sentiment ranks second, with 21.39\% hard samples and 3.37\% all-fail cases. In contrast, the finance datasets-particularly Finance-All-Agree-contain relatively few universally difficult examples. This helps explain why the finance corpora benefit the most from aggregation: there are enough disagreements to exploit, but comparatively fewer samples on which the entire pool fails together.

The qualitative analysis of the hardest samples indicates that the most problematic cases are linguistically and semantically non-trivial. In TweetEval-Irony and TweetEval-Sentiment, many all-fail examples rely on polarity reversal, sarcasm, understatement, or sentiment expressed through pragmatics rather than explicit lexical cues. In Yelp-Full, the hardest reviews are typically long, mixed-polarity texts that combine praise and criticism and require fine-grained rating decisions rather than binary sentiment detection. The Twitter corpus introduces a different source of difficulty: short, noisy, context-poor posts containing placeholders, slang, or fragmentary utterances. These observations suggest that large-scale error overlap is often driven by genuine ambiguity of the input rather than by isolated model weaknesses.

\subsection{Implications for Hybrid System Design}

The error-overlap analysis has direct consequences for the design of hybrid sentiment classifiers. First, high within-family overlap implies diminishing returns from combining multiple similar models, since they frequently contribute redundant mistakes. Second, the existence of hard examples shared across nearly all architectures shows why fixed voting over a static pool cannot always outperform the best individual model: when most models fail on the same sample, majority aggregation has little corrective capacity.

These findings motivate a more selective hybrid strategy than uniform voting. The results reported in Section~\ref{sec:cross_ensemble} already suggest that the best model family is dataset-dependent: transformers dominate on the finance corpora, LLMs are strongest on IMDB and TweetEval-Irony, and deep learning models are clearly superior on Twitter. Rather than treating all predictions as equally useful, future hybrid methods should take into account both expected model quality and the degree of error correlation between model families. In this sense, error-overlap analysis is not only descriptive; it helps identify the conditions under which more adaptive hybrid architectures may be worth exploring.
